<h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Is Maternity Pay Taxed in the UK?</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">The short answer: <strong>Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP) is taxed</strong>, but <strong>Maternity Allowance (MA) is not</strong>. This is one of the most important differences between the two benefits, and it can significantly affect how much money actually reaches your bank account.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">How SMP Is Taxed</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">SMP is treated as earnings by HMRC. This means it's subject to:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Income Tax</strong> — at your normal rate (20%, 40%, or 45% depending on your total annual income)</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Employee National Insurance</strong> — at 8% on earnings between £242 and £967 per week</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Your employer deducts these through the PAYE system, just like they would with your normal salary. The amount deducted appears on your payslip each pay period.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">Tax During the 90% Period (Weeks 1-6)</h3></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">During the first 6 weeks, your SMP is 90% of your average weekly earnings. If your normal salary is £35,000, that's about £605.77/week. At this level, you'll pay both Income Tax (20%) and National Insurance (8%) on the amount above the respective thresholds.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">Tax During the Flat-Rate Period (Weeks 7-39)</h3></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Here's where it gets interesting. The flat rate of <strong>£194.32 per week</strong> translates to approximately £9,733 on an annualised basis. This is below the Personal Allowance of £12,570, which means:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>No Income Tax</strong> would be due if this were your only income for the year</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>No National Insurance</strong> because £194.32 is below the NI Primary Threshold of £242/week</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">However, tax is calculated <strong>cumulatively</strong> through PAYE. If you earned your full salary for part of the tax year before starting maternity leave, you may have already used up some of your Personal Allowance.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Why You Might Be Owed a Tax Refund</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Many women on maternity leave are owed a tax refund without realising it. This happens because:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">1.</span><span>Your employer's PAYE system may have been deducting tax as if you'd earn your full salary all year</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">2.</span><span>When your income drops to SMP levels, you've potentially overpaid tax in earlier months</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">3.</span><span>The cumulative tax calculation should adjust this, but it doesn't always happen smoothly</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">How to Check</h3></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Check your payslip</strong>: Look at the cumulative tax figures. If your cumulative tax paid exceeds what's due on your cumulative earnings, you're owed money</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Use your Personal Tax Account</strong>: Log in to HMRC's online service to see your tax position</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Wait for your P60</strong>: At the end of the tax year, your P60 will show your total earnings and tax paid</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Contact HMRC</strong>: Call 0300 200 3300 if you think you've overpaid</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Maternity Allowance — Tax-Free</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Unlike SMP, <a href="/calculators/maternity-allowance/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Maternity Allowance</a> is <strong>completely tax-free</strong>. The £194.32/week maximum goes straight into your bank account with zero deductions. This is because MA is paid by the DWP as a benefit, not by your employer as earnings.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">This means the <strong>take-home difference</strong> between SMP and MA during the flat-rate period is minimal:
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>SMP flat rate: £194.32/week (little or no tax/NI in practice)</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>MA maximum: £194.32/week (guaranteed no tax/NI)</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">The real difference is in weeks 1-6, where SMP at 90% of earnings can be significantly more than the MA maximum.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Tax Codes During Maternity Leave</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Your tax code should remain the same during maternity leave — typically <strong>1257L</strong> for 2026/27. This code gives you the full Personal Allowance of £12,570.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">If you notice your tax code has changed, check with your employer or HMRC. Common reasons for tax code changes during maternity leave:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Company benefits (car, private health) continuing during leave</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Student loan deductions</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Outstanding tax from previous years</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Errors in HMRC's records</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">National Insurance Credits</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Even when your SMP is below the NI threshold and you're not paying contributions, you're still protected:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Weeks receiving SMP are treated as <strong>qualifying weeks</strong> for State Pension purposes</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><a href="/calculators/child-benefit/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Child Benefit</a> provides additional NI credits for the claiming parent</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Your NI record won't have gaps during maternity leave</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">This is particularly important for your future State Pension entitlement. Read more about this in our guide to <a href="/calculators/child-benefit/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Child Benefit</a>.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Pension Contributions and Tax</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">During maternity leave, your employer must continue their pension contributions based on your <strong>normal salary</strong> (not your reduced SMP). Your own contributions, however, are based on your actual pay:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>During weeks 1-6 (90% pay): Your pension contribution is based on the higher SMP amount</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>During weeks 7-39 (flat rate): Your contribution is based on £194.32/week</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>During weeks 40-52 (unpaid): Neither employer nor employee contributions are required</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">The employer's continued contributions at the normal rate provide valuable tax-efficient pension growth during maternity leave.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Use Our Calculators</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">To see exactly how tax affects your maternity pay:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><a href="/calculators/take-home-pay/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Take-Home Pay Calculator</a> — see your after-tax maternity pay</span></li>
