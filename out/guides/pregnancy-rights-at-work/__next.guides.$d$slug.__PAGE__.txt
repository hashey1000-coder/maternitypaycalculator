<h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Your Pregnancy Rights at Work</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">From the moment you tell your employer you're pregnant, UK employment law gives you extensive protections. Understanding these rights is essential — they protect you from the day you announce your pregnancy until you return from <a href="/guides/maternity-leave-rights/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">maternity leave</a>.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Protection from Dismissal</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">It is <strong>automatically unfair</strong> to dismiss a woman because she is pregnant, regardless of how long she has worked for the employer. This protection covers:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Dismissal during pregnancy</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Dismissal during maternity leave</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Dismissal because you took or sought to take maternity leave</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Redundancy — if you are selected for redundancy during pregnancy or maternity leave because of your pregnancy, this is unlawful</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">If you're made redundant while pregnant or on maternity leave, you have the right to be offered any suitable alternative vacancy that exists, and you have priority over other employees.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Risk Assessments</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Your employer has a legal duty to carry out a <strong>workplace risk assessment</strong> once they know you're pregnant. This must identify any risks to your health or your baby's health, including:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Heavy lifting or physical work</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Standing or sitting for long periods</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Exposure to chemicals, radiation, or infectious diseases</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Night work or excessive working hours</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Stress levels</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">If a risk is identified, your employer must:
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">1.</span><span><strong>Adjust your working conditions</strong> to remove the risk, or</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">2.</span><span><strong>Offer you suitable alternative work</strong> on the same terms, or</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">3.</span><span><strong>Suspend you on full pay</strong> if no safe alternative can be found</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Paid Time Off for Antenatal Care</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">You have the right to <strong>paid time off</strong> for antenatal appointments, no matter how long you've worked for your employer. This includes:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>All appointments with your midwife or doctor</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Antenatal classes recommended by your midwife</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Hospital appointments related to your pregnancy</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Your employer can ask to see your appointment card after the first appointment, but they cannot refuse you time off or require you to make up the hours.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Partners who are expecting a baby (including same-sex partners) also have the right to unpaid time off to attend up to <strong>two antenatal appointments</strong>.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Pregnancy Discrimination</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Pregnancy discrimination is unlawful under the <strong>Equality Act 2010</strong>. It includes:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Treating you less favourably because of your pregnancy</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Failing to offer you training, promotions, or opportunities</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Changing your role, responsibilities, or working conditions negatively</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Making derogatory comments about your pregnancy</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Failing to carry out a risk assessment</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Pressuring you about your return date or work plans</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Pregnancy discrimination doesn't require you to compare yourself to anyone else — if you're treated unfavourably because of pregnancy, it's discrimination. Full stop. For more detail, read our dedicated guide on <a href="/guides/pregnancy-discrimination-at-work/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">pregnancy discrimination at work</a>. If your employer is making your position redundant, see our guide on <a href="/guides/redundancy-during-pregnancy-maternity/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">redundancy during pregnancy and maternity leave</a> to understand your enhanced protections.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Flexible Working During Pregnancy</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">While there's no specific right to flexible working during pregnancy, your employer should make reasonable adjustments if your pregnancy is causing difficulties. This might include:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Allowing you to start later or finish earlier</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Providing more frequent breaks</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Allowing you to work from home</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Reducing your workload or adjusting duties</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">If you have a pregnancy-related health condition, your employer may also have duties under disability discrimination law to make reasonable adjustments.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Pregnancy-Related Sickness</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">If you're off sick with a pregnancy-related illness:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>In the first 36 weeks: You should receive your normal sick pay (SSP or contractual)</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>In the <strong>last 4 weeks before your due date</strong>: If you're off with a pregnancy-related illness, your employer can trigger your maternity leave automatically</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Your pregnancy-related absences should <strong>not</strong> be counted in any sickness absence records or used against you in disciplinary proceedings</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Health and Safety Protections</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Specific health and safety regulations protect pregnant workers:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Night work</strong>: If a medical certificate says night work could harm your health, your employer must offer daytime work or suspend you on full pay</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Rest facilities</strong>: Your employer must provide suitable rest facilities for pregnant employees</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Working hours</strong>: The Working Time Regulations limit your hours, and these protections are particularly important during pregnancy</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">What to Do If Your Rights Are Breached</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">If you believe your pregnancy rights are being violated:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">1.</span><span><strong>Raise it informally</strong> with your manager or HR department</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">2.</span><span><strong>Put your concerns in writing</strong> — create a paper trail</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">3.</span><span><strong>Contact ACAS</strong> on 0300 123 1100 for free, confidential advice</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">4.</span><span><strong>Join a trade union</strong> if you haven't already — they can provide support and representation</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">5.</span><span><strong>Seek legal advice</strong> from a solicitor specialising in employment law</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">6.</span><span><strong>Make a formal grievance</strong> through your employer's grievance procedure</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">7.</span><span><strong>Employment Tribunal</strong> — as a last resort, you can bring a claim (the time limit is usually 3 months less a day from the act of discrimination)</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Your Maternity Pay Rights</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Understanding your financial entitlements is equally important:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><a href="/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Calculate your SMP</a> — see how much you'll receive</span></li>
