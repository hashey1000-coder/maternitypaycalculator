<h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Your Maternity Leave Entitlement</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Every pregnant employee in the UK has the right to <strong>52 weeks of maternity leave</strong>, regardless of how long you've worked for your employer. This is split into:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Ordinary Maternity Leave (OML)</strong>: First 26 weeks</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>Additional Maternity Leave (AML)</strong>: Last 26 weeks</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">You don't have to take all 52 weeks, but you <strong>must</strong> take at least <strong>2 weeks after birth</strong> (4 weeks if you work in a factory).</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Key Dates You Need to Know</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">The Qualifying Week</h3>
The 15th week before your expected week of childbirth. This is crucial because:
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>You need 26 weeks continuous employment by this point for SMP</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>You should notify your employer by this week</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">Notification Deadline</h3>
You must tell your employer about your pregnancy by the <strong>15th week before your due date</strong>. You need to provide:
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Confirmation that you're pregnant</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Your expected due date</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>When you want maternity leave to start</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Use our <a href="/calculators/maternity-leave-planner/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Maternity Leave Planner</a> to calculate all your key dates.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Protection During Pregnancy</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">You Cannot Be Dismissed for Being Pregnant</h3>
It is automatically unfair dismissal to sack someone because they're pregnant or on maternity leave. This protection starts from <strong>day one</strong> of employment.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">Risk Assessments</h3>
Your employer must carry out a risk assessment of your workplace and role. If risks are identified, they must:
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">1.</span><span>Adjust your working conditions</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">2.</span><span>Offer you suitable alternative work</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">3.</span><span>Suspend you on full pay if no alternative exists</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">Time Off for Antenatal Care</h3>
You have the right to <strong>paid time off</strong> for antenatal appointments, including:
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Medical appointments</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Antenatal classes recommended by your doctor</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Relaxation classes</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Parent-craft classes</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Your Rights During Maternity Leave</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">First 26 Weeks (OML)</h3>
During OML, you're entitled to:
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>All your normal contractual benefits (except salary) — e.g., company car, pension contributions, gym membership</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Continued accrual of annual leave</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Protection from redundancy (priority for suitable alternative roles)</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">Last 26 Weeks (AML)</h3>
During AML, your rights are the same, but some non-pay benefits may not continue (check your contract).</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">Annual Leave</h3>
You continue to accrue holiday during your entire maternity leave. Many employers allow you to:
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Take annual leave before maternity leave starts</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Add annual leave onto the end of maternity leave</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>This can significantly extend your time off</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Keeping in Touch (KIT) Days</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">You can work up to <strong>10 KIT days</strong> during your maternity leave without ending your leave or losing SMP. These are voluntary — neither you nor your employer can insist on them.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">KIT days are great for:
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Staying connected with colleagues</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Attending training or conferences</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Easing back into work gradually</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Enhanced Redundancy Protection</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Since April 2024, pregnant employees and those on maternity leave have <strong>enhanced protection</strong> against redundancy in the UK. This protection now extends to:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>The entire period of your pregnancy, from the point you notify your employer</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>The full duration of your maternity leave</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><strong>18 months after your baby's birth</strong> (even after you have returned to work)</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">During this protected period, if your role is made redundant, your employer must offer you any suitable alternative vacancy <strong>before</strong> offering it to other employees. This is a significant strengthening of the previous rules, which only protected employees during maternity leave itself.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">If you believe you have been unfairly selected for redundancy because of your pregnancy or maternity leave, read our guide on <a href="/guides/redundancy-during-pregnancy-maternity/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">redundancy during pregnancy and maternity</a> for advice on your next steps.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Pregnancy Discrimination</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">It is unlawful for your employer to treat you unfavourably because of your pregnancy or maternity leave. <a href="/guides/pregnancy-discrimination-at-work/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Pregnancy discrimination at work</a> can take many forms, including:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Being overlooked for promotion or training opportunities</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Receiving negative performance reviews related to pregnancy absence</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Being pressured to return to work earlier than you wish</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Having your role changed or downgraded without consultation</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Being made to feel unwelcome when you return</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Being dismissed or made redundant because of your pregnancy</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Protection against pregnancy discrimination applies from <strong>day one</strong> of employment — you do not need any minimum length of service. If you experience discrimination, keep a written record of all incidents with dates and details, and seek advice promptly.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Returning to Work</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">After OML (First 26 Weeks)</h3>
You have the right to return to the <strong>exact same job</strong> with the same terms and conditions, including your seniority, pension rights, and any other benefits. Your employer cannot change your role, responsibilities, or working conditions without your agreement.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">After AML (Last 26 Weeks)</h3>
You have the right to return to the same job. If that is not reasonably practicable (for example, due to a genuine restructure), your employer must offer you a <strong>suitable alternative role</strong> on terms and conditions no less favourable than your original position.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-800 mt-6 mb-2">Flexible Working</h3>
You have the right to <strong>request flexible working</strong> from day one of employment. Your employer must deal with your request in a reasonable manner and respond within two months. They can only refuse for one of eight specific business reasons set out in law. Read our full guide on <a href="/guides/flexible-working-after-maternity-leave/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">flexible working after maternity leave</a> for tips on making a successful request.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">For more practical advice on your return, see our complete guide to <a href="/guides/returning-to-work-after-maternity/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">returning to work after maternity leave</a>.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Shared Parental Leave</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">If you want to share your leave with your partner, you can convert your maternity leave into <strong><a href="/guides/shared-parental-leave-guide/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Shared Parental Leave (SPL)</a></strong>. This allows:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Your partner to take some of the remaining leave and pay</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>You to take leave in up to three separate blocks rather than one continuous period</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span>Both parents to be off work at the same time</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">SPL can provide greater flexibility for your family, but it requires careful planning. Your partner must also meet employment and earnings criteria to qualify. Use our <a href="/calculators/shared-parental-leave/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Shared Parental Leave Calculator</a> to explore your options, or read our comprehensive <a href="/guides/shared-parental-leave-guide/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Shared Parental Leave guide</a> for full details on eligibility and how to apply.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">What To Do If Your Rights Are Violated</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">If your employer is not respecting your maternity leave rights in the UK:</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">1.</span><span><strong>Raise a formal grievance</strong> with your employer in writing, keeping a copy for your records</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">2.</span><span><strong>Contact ACAS</strong> for free, confidential advice (0300 123 1100) — ACAS can also provide early conciliation before a tribunal claim</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">3.</span><span><strong>Seek legal advice</strong> from a specialist employment lawyer — many offer free initial consultations</span></li>
<li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-600 font-semibold">4.</span><span><strong>Make an employment tribunal claim</strong> within 3 months (minus one day) of the discriminatory act</span></li></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Keep records of all conversations, emails, and incidents — these will be essential if you need to take your case further.</p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><h2 class="text-xl font-bold text-slate-900 mt-8 mb-3">Plan Your Maternity Leave</h2></p><p class="text-slate-600 leading-relaxed mb-3"><li class="flex items-start gap-2 ml-4"><span class="text-violet-400 mt-1">•</span><span><a href="/" class="text-violet-600 hover:underline font-medium">Calculate your SMP</a> with our free maternity pay calculator</span></li>
